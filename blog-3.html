RETURN OF HUMMING WHALE

  • Home
  • /
  • Blog
May 23, 2024 1 Comment Kristellar Team

HummingBad, a highly sophisticated and well-developed Android malware, has made a significant impact by employing a chain-attack tactic and a rootkit to gain comprehensive control over infected devices. This malware has been estimated to have compromised over 85 million devices globally and was recently discovered in 46 new applications, including 20 that had managed to bypass Google’s security checks and appear in the official Play Store. HummingBad is currently the most prominent Android malware, responsible for 72% of all mobile infections.

The evolution of HummingBad has led to a new variant named HummingWhale, which introduces significant operational changes. Unlike its predecessor, HummingWhale does not use a rootkit but instead utilizes a tool called DroidPlugin for running virtual machines on Android. This shift allows the malware to display unwanted ads and, when users attempt to close them, open a virtual machine to install the advertised app. This strategy enables the malware to profit from pay-per-install schemes without cluttering the device’s app list.

HummingBad's evolution into HummingWhale underscores the need for robust malware defenses against increasingly sophisticated attacks.

The Chinese cybercriminal group Yingmob, responsible for HummingBad, has used its persistent rootkit to establish control over millions of Android devices, generating fraudulent ad revenue and installing malicious apps. This financial independence has allowed them to enhance their malware capabilities, create powerful botnets, and conduct targeted attacks, further expanding their criminal activities.

In addition to HummingWhale, a new malware family called LeifAccess or Shopper exploits Android’s accessibility features. This malware creates accounts, downloads apps, and posts reviews while disguising itself as a legitimate Settings icon. Once the user clicks on this icon, a fake error message prompts the user to uninstall the app, but in reality, this action completes the installation and hides the malware, making it challenging for users to detect and remove it.

The year 2024 has also brought about the emergence of a sophisticated new threat known as QuantumPhish. This advanced phishing attack leverages quantum computing to crack encryption algorithms in real-time. QuantumPhish employs sophisticated social engineering tactics to create nearly undetectable fake banking websites. Once users enter their credentials, the malware rapidly decrypts and hijacks sensitive information, posing a significant threat to traditional cryptographic methods.

As AI advancements continue to fuel the rise of undetectable malware, spyware, and ransomware, attackers are increasingly targeting legitimate developer accounts to infiltrate widely-used apps with strong reputations. During the COVID-19 pandemic, two variants of a compromised app saw over 600,000 installs. This evolving threat landscape underscores the critical need for advanced threat detection and prevention solutions that can protect both modern and legacy smartphones from such cyber threats.
